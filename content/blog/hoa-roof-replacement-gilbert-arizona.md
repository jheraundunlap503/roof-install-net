# HOA Roof Replacement in Gilbert, Arizona: What You Need to Approve Before You Start

**TLDR:** Replacing a roof in a Gilbert or East Valley HOA community requires written approval from the Architectural Review Committee before work begins — not after. Material restrictions are real: most communities require specific tile profiles, colors, and finishes that match the neighborhood standard. Skipping the approval process can result in fines, a mandatory redo, and a lien on your property. This guide explains the process, the timelines, and how to avoid the mistakes that cost homeowners in Gilbert and Chandler thousands in unnecessary rework.

---

Gilbert is one of the most HOA-dense cities in Arizona. More than 60% of Gilbert residents live in communities governed by a homeowners association, and East Valley HOAs tend to be active — meaning approval requirements for exterior work are enforced, not just written into the CC&Rs and forgotten.

Roof replacement is among the most regulated exterior changes you can make. Unlike paint color, which affects curb appeal, a roof affects the structural character of the whole community. HOAs in Gilbert, Chandler, Queen Creek, and Mesa treat material, color, and profile matching as non-negotiable — and several have contractors pre-approved to do the work.

Knowing the process before you call a roofer saves weeks of delays and protects you from the cost of doing the job twice.

---

## Does my HOA control what material I can use for my roof replacement?

Yes, in most Gilbert and East Valley HOA communities. Governing documents — the CC&Rs (Covenants, Conditions, and Restrictions) and the community Architectural Standards — typically specify the approved roofing material (usually concrete tile or a specific tile profile), color range (often referenced by manufacturer color code, not just a general description), and in some cases the manufacturer.

If your community was built with S-curve concrete tile in a specific blend, your replacement must match that profile. Installing flat tile, clay tile in a different shade, or asphalt shingle — even temporarily — can trigger a violation notice and a requirement to redo the work at your expense. See our [tile roof replacement guide for Gilbert](/services/tile-roof-replacement-gilbert-az/) for cost ranges and what the ARC submission process looks like from the contractor side.

## What is the ARC and how does the approval process work?

The ARC (Architectural Review Committee) is the HOA subcommittee that reviews exterior modification requests. Before any roofing work begins, you submit an ARC application that includes:

- A description of the work (full replacement or tile lift-and-relay)
- The material specification: manufacturer, product name, color code, profile
- Your licensed contractor's information and ROC license number
- A start date

The ARC reviews the submission and has a window defined in the CC&Rs — typically 30–45 days — to approve, deny, or request additional information. Most straightforward replacements using approved materials are approved within 2–3 weeks. Denials typically come when the submitted material doesn't match the community standard.

## How long does HOA approval take for a roof replacement in Gilbert?

Standard ARC review in Gilbert and Chandler communities runs 2–4 weeks if the application is complete. Incomplete applications — missing contractor credentials, wrong color code, no start date — restart the clock.

Factor in 4–6 weeks of lead time total: 1 week to prepare the application, 2–4 weeks for ARC review, and then scheduling with your contractor. If your roof is damaged and you need work done urgently, read the emergency provisions below before assuming you can start without approval.

## Can I start roof repairs immediately if there is storm damage?

Most Gilbert HOA governing documents include an emergency repair provision: work necessary to prevent further property damage may begin without prior ARC approval, provided you notify the HOA within 24–72 hours and submit a full ARC application within 5–7 business days. The key word is "emergency." A leak that can be temporarily tarped does not typically qualify. A structural compromise or active interior water intrusion usually does.

Get the emergency provision language from your HOA management company before assuming it applies to your situation. In practice, a quick call to the management company explaining the damage and asking whether emergency provisions apply takes 10 minutes and protects you if there is any dispute later.

## What happens if I replace my roof without HOA approval?

Three things can happen, and none are cheap:

**1. Violation notice and fine.** Most East Valley HOAs can issue fines of $25–$100 per day for ongoing violations, which accumulate until the violation is resolved.

**2. Mandatory removal or replacement.** If you installed non-compliant material, the HOA can require you to remove it and replace it with the approved material at your expense. This means paying twice for the same job.

**3. Lien on your property.** Unpaid HOA fines and violation fees can become liens against your home, which must be satisfied before you can sell or refinance.

The most common version of this problem in Gilbert: a homeowner hires a contractor who says they "deal with HOAs all the time," starts work without submitting paperwork, and installs a tile product that is close to — but not an exact match for — the community standard. The HOA's ARC inspector flags the color discrepancy. The homeowner is now on the hook for a full redo with the correct tile.

## What should a legitimate HOA roofing contract include?

A contract for HOA work in Gilbert or Chandler should specifically reference the community's ARC approval requirements and include:

- The exact material specification matching what was submitted to the ARC
- Confirmation that the contractor holds an active R-42 license (verify at [roc.az.gov](https://roc.az.gov))
- A written clause stating work will not begin until written ARC approval is received
- Documentation of the HOA approval letter or approval number

Any contractor unwilling to put the ARC approval as a precondition to start is telling you they plan to start before approval. That is a problem you will own, not them.

## Does the HOA or the homeowner pay for roof replacement in Gilbert?

In single-family home communities (which make up the majority of HOA neighborhoods in Gilbert), roof replacement is the homeowner's responsibility. The HOA governs what material is used and when work can begin — it does not fund it.

In some attached-unit communities (townhomes and condos), the HOA may be responsible for the roof as common-area infrastructure. Check your CC&Rs for the specific definition of what constitutes "common area" versus "limited common area" in your community.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Does Gilbert, AZ require a building permit for roof replacement in addition to HOA approval?**
Yes. Roof replacement in Gilbert requires both an HOA ARC approval and a Town of Gilbert building permit. These are separate processes. Your contractor pulls the permit; you obtain the ARC approval. A legitimate contractor will handle the permit side automatically. Verify the permit was pulled by checking the Town of Gilbert online permit portal before work begins.

**My neighbor replaced their roof last year. Can I use their contractor and material without going through ARC?**
No. Each replacement requires its own ARC application, even if the material is identical. The ARC application documents that your specific roof was approved — which matters if there is ever a dispute about whether the work was authorized. Your neighbor's approved application does not cover your home.

**What if the ARC denies my roof replacement application in Gilbert?**
You have the right to appeal within a timeframe specified in your CC&Rs — typically 30 days. The appeal goes to the full HOA board. The most common grounds for denial are material mismatch and missing documentation. Fix the specific issue the ARC cited and resubmit. If you believe the denial is unreasonable, Arizona law allows homeowners to pursue mediation before litigation under the Arizona Planned Community Act (A.R.S. Title 33, Chapter 16).

**Can I switch from tile to shingle in a Gilbert HOA community?**
Only if the community's Architectural Standards explicitly permit shingle as an approved material. In most Gilbert HOA communities with concrete tile as the standard, shingle is not permitted as a replacement material regardless of cost or preference. If you need a more affordable option, inquire about a tile lift-and-relay — replacing just the underlayment while reusing existing tiles — which can cost $4.00–$6.50/sq ft compared to $9.65–$14.00 for full tile replacement.

**How do I find out exactly what material my HOA requires?**
Request a copy of the Architectural Standards from your HOA management company. This document specifically names the approved materials, manufacturers, and color codes. If your community is large enough to have a developer-era specification sheet, request that as well. Some Gilbert communities have updated their approved product list since original construction — meaning the tile your neighbors used 5 years ago may have been replaced by a different approved product today.
