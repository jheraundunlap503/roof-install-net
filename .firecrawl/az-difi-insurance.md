## Basic Home Insurance

Home insurance is a form of property insurance that covers losses and damages to an individual's residence along with furnishings and other assets in the home. It also offers protection against liability for accidents that injure other people or damage their property.

### Types of Home Policies

- **Standard (HO-3):** The most common policy. Protects your house, adjacent structures, and personal property against a broad range of covered perils.
- **Renters (HO-4):** Designed for tenants. Protects your furniture and personal property inside a rented unit, and provides liability coverage. Does not cover the building.
- **Condo (HO-6):** Typically covers the interior walls, fixtures, and personal property ("walls-in"), while the HOA's master policy covers the exterior and common areas.
- **Manufactured (HO-7):** Similar to standard homeowners insurance but specifically tailored to the unique risks and valuation of mobile and manufactured homes.

### Home Insurance vs. Home Warranties

Consumers frequently confuse home insurance with home warranties. It is important to understand the difference:

- **Home Insurance** covers sudden, accidental damage and catastrophic losses (e.g., fires, windstorms, theft, and liability claims).
- **Home Warranties** (often called service contracts) cover the repair or replacement of home systems and appliances that break down over time due to normal wear and tear (e.g., an AC unit failure or a refrigerator breakdown).

### Self-Insuring / Dropping Coverage on Paid-Off Homes

Some homeowners who have paid off their homes consider forgoing insurance to save money. However, homeowners without insurance are completely self-insured, meaning they are fully financially at risk in the event of a catastrophic loss (like a fire). If an individual cannot afford insurance, they likely cannot afford to lose their house. DIFI strongly encourages maintaining insurance to ensure financial resiliency.

## Determining Coverage Needs

More coverage generally means you will have less to pay out of your own pocket if disaster strikes. You must determine the amount you can financially afford to lose or need to receive in order to rebuild. Your lender may require you to cover the house for at least the amount of the mortgage, but this may be too little or too much for your individual circumstances. You are not required to purchase insurance from the specific insurer recommended by your lender.

### Home Replacement Coverage

If you think the cost to replace or rebuild your home has increased because of upgrades, additions, or rising labor and material costs, ask your agent if you have enough coverage. Coverage needs are based on **replacement cost, not market value**. The cost of labor and materials may increase regardless of real estate market value.

### Personal Property & Appraisals

When you receive an inheritance, buy new electronics, or sell expensive items, it is a great time to adjust your coverage limits. High-value items (jewelry, firearms, artwork) are hard to track after a loss. An appraisal provides a record of existence and replacement cost. Maintain a home inventory list to aid potential claims.

### Insurance Deductibles and Premiums

Talk to your insurance agent to get help understanding whether a change in an insurance deductible will save you money. Policyholders may significantly reduce their premium payments by raising their deductible (the amount paid out-of-pocket for a claim). This option may not be ideal for everyone, and asking your agent to explain your insurance policy to you will help you determine what deductible and premium payment will suit your needs and fit in your budget.

## Key Policy Terms & Types

There are two primary ways to look at the value of a residence and the contents of the residence (personal property). It is important to know which kind of insurance coverage you have.

### Replacement Cost

The amount necessary to replace or rebuild your home or repair damages with materials of similar kind and quality **without deducting for depreciation**. Insurance policies often restrict the circumstances for paying a claim under replacement cost.

### Actual Cash Value (ACV)

The sum of money required at the time of the loss to repair or replace the property destroyed, **less an amount for depreciation** (Replacement Cost minus Depreciation). Most standard policies cover personal property on an ACV basis, but replacement cost coverage can often be purchased.

### All Risk vs. Named Peril Policies

Be sure you know which type of policy you have and the kinds of loss that the policy covers.

- **"All Risk" Policies:** Most homeowner insurance policies sold are "all risk" which means they cover the largest number of causes of loss (freezing, collapse, fire, windstorm, smoke, etc.). However, there are still some exclusions in all homeowner policies (e.g., flood, war, neglect).
- **"Named Peril" Policies:** These policies limit the covered causes of loss to those specifically listed or “named” in the policy. Usually, the policy will cover the residence only when a specific kind of loss occurs.

### Glossary of Additional Terms

Additional Living Expense (ALE)Coverage for the necessary increase in expenses you may incur if your home is damaged by an insured event, so that your household can maintain its normal standard of living (e.g., motel costs, dining out, or storing property).

Increased Cost to Changes in Building OrdinancesCoverage for areas where building code changes require additional and usually expensive additions (e.g., sprinkler systems, new standards for plumbing or wiring) when rebuilding a damaged residence.

Inflation Guard ProtectionA policy provision that automatically increases the amount of dwelling insurance to account for inflation in replacement costs, helping prevent your home from being underinsured.

Medical Payments InsuranceCoverage that pays reasonable expenses for others accidentally injured on your premises, regardless of who is at fault. It does not apply to your own injuries, family members living with you, or intentional acts.

Personal Liability InsuranceProtects you and family members who live with you against a claim or lawsuit resulting from bodily injury or property damage to others for which you become legally obligated to pay.

### Policy Changes and Notices

As a homeowner insurance policy owner, you are entitled to notice of any change in the terms of your policy coverage. You have the responsibility to read all notices and information you receive from your insurance company.

## DIFI’s Authority to Affect the Cost of Insurance

DIFI has limited authority to directly influence insurance rates. In Arizona, insurance rates operate on a **"use and file"** system—meaning an insurer can start using a rate change immediately, and then file that change with DIFI within 30 days.

DIFI examines rate changes based on three statutory standards to ensure they are not:

- **Excessive:** By law, a rate cannot be found to be excessive if adequate competition exists within the state. Currently, there are over 100 companies licensed in Arizona that may offer homeowners insurance policies. The statute presumes that rates are not excessive if the market is competitive.
- **Inadequate:** Rates must be sufficient to ensure an insurer's solvency. It is critical for an insurer to have enough capital to fulfill its obligation to pay claims.
- **Unfairly Discriminatory:** Premiums must be based on relative risk, and rates for similar risks must be treated the same. Unfair discrimination includes charging different rates for identical risks or denying coverage based on protected classes like race or religion.

## Premium Increases & Data Trends

With home insurance premiums rising in Arizona and nationwide, consumers living in high-risk or disaster-prone areas are seeing significant spikes. The price insurance companies charge is determined by the likelihood that a claim will be made and the estimated cost to handle that claim.

### Rising Construction & Labor Costs

Nationally, construction costs have increased over **30% within the last 5 years**. In Arizona specifically, average reconstruction costs for residential structures increased **6% from September 2022 to September 2023** (according to Verisk data). Construction costs vary based on the type of residence, building materials, local climate, and building codes. While the cost of homeowners insurance has not always kept pace with the cost of construction, the trend indicates an acceleration of homeowners insurance price increases.

![Graph showing rising cost of construction materials and labor versus insurance costs](https://difi.az.gov/sites/default/files/2024-08/Screenshot%202024-07-23%20095832.png)

### Homeowner Premium Trends (HO-3 Policies)

Based on the most current data from the National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC), average premiums for HO-3 policies (the most common category of homeowners insurance) have trended upwards nationwide from 2018 through 2021. While states like California have seen premiums outpace the national average, Arizona and the nation as a whole are experiencing steady upward trends.

![Graph showing cumulative percentage change in average HO-3 premiums](https://difi.az.gov/sites/default/files/2024-08/Screenshot%202024-07-23%20095448.png)

### Issues Facing Arizona Homeowners

- Insurance premiums are increasing across the state, not just in wildfire-prone areas.
- Consumers in wildfire-prone areas may experience non-renewals and struggle to find replacement coverage.
- Mortgage affordability and availability are heavily impacted by insurance costs.
- Increased flood risks often follow wildfires (post-fire flooding).

### Reinsurance and Out-of-State Catastrophes

Insurers offering homeowners insurance in Arizona are required to base their premiums on loss experiences specific to Arizona. This means an insurer cannot attempt to recoup localized losses from another state by passing rate increases to Arizona policyholders. However, insurers purchase their own insurance—called "reinsurance"—to protect themselves from insolvency after catastrophic payouts. When the global risk of perils (like wildfires) increases, the cost of reinsurance goes up across the board, and insurers pass these increased operational costs along to policyholders.

### Rethinking How We Use Insurance

We are rapidly transitioning away from the paradigm many long-time homeowners have held about insurance. With the hardening of the insurance market in today’s environment, homeowners insurance may be best reserved for **catastrophic events**—such as a fire or severe damage resulting in tens of thousands of dollars in loss—rather than small, manageable repairs.

## Cancellations & Non-Renewals

While often used interchangeably by consumers, there is an important legal difference between a **cancellation** and a **non-renewal**.

### Cancellations

A cancellation is the termination of a policy by the insurer _during_ the active policy period. Insurers are only required to provide **5 days' notice**. Cancellations are relatively uncommon and can only be executed for specific statutory reasons, such as non-payment of premium, fraud, or a substantial change in risk.

### Non-Renewals

A non-renewal is an insurer's decision not to renew a policy for the successive term. Non-renewals are much more common as insurers attempt to reduce risk exposure. Insurers are required to provide at least **30 days' notice** of a non-renewal before the end of the policy period.

### Disputing and Remedying Non-Renewals

If an insurer decides not to renew your policy due to a specific curable condition related to your property (such as having overgrown brush within 10 feet of your home), they must notify you of the condition and give you an opportunity to remedy it. If you fix the condition within the specified time (usually 30 days) in a manner acceptable to the insurer, the policy must be renewed.

If you believe your insurer is making a non-renewal decision based on inaccurate or outdated information (e.g., incorrect square footage or roof age), contact the insurer immediately to request the specific data used for the determination and provide them with the corrected data.

## ISO Ratings & Risk Mitigation

The reduction of risk is the necessary element to lowering insurance premiums in our state. Insurers closely evaluate the specific risks associated with your property's location and condition.

### Wildfire Risks: The WUI and ISO Public Protection Classifications

Factors influencing wildfire risk to homes include **Wildland-Urban Interface/Intermix (WUI) Encroachment** (development adjacent to or within forested areas), local building codes, and the region's dry winter and monsoon climate.

To quantify these risks, insurers commonly use the **Insurance Services Office (ISO) Public Protection Classification (PPC)**. Classes range from 1 (best) to 10 (worst) and are determined by a community’s fire prevention and suppression capabilities, based on:

1. Fire departments and fire districts
2. Water resources
3. Emergency communications
4. Community risk reduction efforts

Currently, **38 Arizona communities** (within the jurisdiction of a graded fire department) are classified as 8B or worse, indicating a higher relative wildfire risk. Insurers typically will not insure homes graded as Class 10. Keep in mind that an individual property may have a different classification than its community due to its specific distance from a fire station or water source.

![PPC Classification Chart](https://difi.az.gov/sites/default/files/2024-08/Screenshot%202024-04-10%20132049.png)

### Personal & Community Mitigation Efforts

- **Create Defensible Space:** Keep your yard clean of flammable debris and understand how landscaping choices affect the way fire or embers interact with your home. Create a defensible space around your home and outbuildings.
- **Use Fire-Resistant Materials:** Utilize fire-resistant building materials for roofs, decks, and outbuildings.
- **Join Community Programs:** Become involved in community mitigation organizations such as [Firewise USA® ⧉(opens in a new tab)](https://www.nfpa.org/education-and-research/wildfire/firewise-usa/firewise-usa-sites) or FEMA preparedness programs.

### The Resiliency and Mitigation Council

Headed by DIFI, the [Resiliency and Mitigation Council](https://difi.az.gov/resiliency-and-mitigation-council) investigates the availability and affordability of homeowners insurance in wildfire-prone areas of Arizona, the impact of natural disasters on insurance availability, and strategies that local communities may adopt to mitigate the risk of property loss.

## Storms & Disasters

### Preparation & Coverage Limitations

The best time to prepare for what to do after a storm or disaster is before the event happens. While home insurance may cover damage to your home caused by fire, wind, rain, snow, lightning, or hail, your policy may have specific limitations and exclusions.

### Flood & Water Damage Exclusions

Damage caused by **flooding** is not covered by standard homeowners insurance. To cover your home for flood damage, you must purchase a separate flood insurance policy through the National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP).

If you have water damage to the inside of your home from rain or snow, your policy may not cover it unless the rain or snow entered through a roof or wall damaged by wind or hail. Additionally, water damage resulting in mold and rot is typically excluded, though some insurers offer optional coverage endorsements.

### Common Storm Damages

- **Falling Objects:** If a tree on your property falls on your house, you can file a claim under your homeowners policy. If it falls on your vehicle, you can file a claim under your auto policy (if you carry comprehensive coverage).
- **Roof Damage or Collapse:** Homeowners policies generally cover only the portion of a roof that was damaged by, or collapsed from, falling objects, rain, snow, or wind.
- **Hail Damage:** Homeowners policies generally cover hail damage to your home and personal property. Hail damage to your vehicle is typically covered by your auto policy (if you carry comprehensive coverage).

### What to Do When Damage Occurs

Document all damage immediately by taking photographs or video recordings. Contact your insurance company **before** you begin to repair, replace, clean, or dispose of your property so they have the opportunity to confirm and evaluate the damage. If you clean or repair property before this evaluation, your claim could be denied.

Your policy may require you to take steps to prevent further damage (e.g., boarding up a broken window, putting a tarp on a roof), even if coverage is unconfirmed. If emergency repairs or cleanup are absolutely necessary before an adjuster arrives, ask your insurer what documentation they will accept, and keep all receipts and photo records of the work performed.

### Beware of Post-Disaster Contractor Scams

After a community suffers widespread damage, opportunistic workers often show up uninvited with offers to inspect and fix the damage. These scams victimize homeowners and insurers through deficient work, inflated bills, and out-of-pocket costs declined by the insurance company.

**Before signing any agreement** with an individual representing themselves as a contractor or independent adjuster:

- Contact your insurer for an official inspection and a referral to a trusted contractor.
- Verify contractor licenses at the [Arizona Registrar of Contractors ⧉(opens in a new tab)](https://roc.az.gov/).
- Verify adjuster licenses at the [DIFI License Search](https://difi.az.gov/license-search).

### Deciding to File a Claim

When deciding whether to file a claim or pay for repairs yourself, consider your insurance claim history. Insurers evaluate your history periodically to determine premium rates and renewal eligibility. If you think the repair costs might be less than your policy deductible, it may be better to pay out-of-pocket to avoid potential rate increases or non-renewal. Filing a claim should be the last consideration, as insurance is primarily intended to address catastrophic damage that cannot be covered out-of-pocket. If you call your insurer simply to ask questions about your coverage, explicitly state that you are making an "inquiry" and not filing a claim.

## Dog Breeds & Liability (A.R.S. § 20-1510)

Under Arizona law ( [A.R.S. § 20-1510 ⧉(opens in a new tab)](https://www.azleg.gov/legtext/55leg/2R/laws/0243.pdf)), effective June 30, 2023, the actual or perceived breed (or mixture of breeds) of a dog may not be used as the **sole factor** for underwriting or actuarial processes, or for gathering information via questionnaires or surveys.

### Applicability

The law applies to the entirety of all new and renewing homeowners and renters policies (HO1 through HO8), including any riders or endorsements. It does **not** apply to commercial, excess, or umbrella liability policies. If an insurer's policy documents or applications reference "breed," they must be consistent with the definition of breed in A.R.S. § 20-1510.

### Underwriting, Rating, and Coverage Limitations

While an insurer cannot decline, non-renew, cancel, surcharge, or increase premiums based solely on a dog's breed, they are permitted to take adverse actions based on the possession of _any_ dog or based on factors unrelated to breed. For example, an insurer may:

- Increase premiums, reduce liability coverage, or decline an applicant for the mere possession of _any_ dog (applied universally, not targeting specific breeds).
- Exclude "Animal Liability" coverage entirely for all animals.
- Exclude coverage for a _specific_ dog with a documented history of biting or aggression (similar to an excluded driver on an auto policy).
- Impose underwriting conditions unrelated to breed (e.g., requiring a 6-foot fence for any dog weighing more than 40 pounds).
- Base underwriting decisions on a dog's individual bite history, age, or the total number of dogs in the household.

### Application Questions & Hybrids

Insurers are allowed to ask for the breed of a dog on applications, but the breed cannot be the sole factor used to make adverse underwriting decisions. Wolf or wild animal-dog hybrids fall under the statutory definition of "breed" and are subject to these same regulations.
